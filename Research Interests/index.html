Topological Orders

There is a fundamental problem in condensed matter physics: How to describe and classify different phases of matter? In Landau’s paradigm, phases (or orders) are described by symmetry. It turns out that there is a new kind of order beyond the paradigm, which is named topological order . The absence of symmetry doesn't imply topological orders are trivial or messy; on the contrary, they exhibit numerous extremely intriguing features.

One interesting feature is the existence of topological excitations (anyons). They cannot be created or annihilated by local operators; moreover, their properties are invariant under local perturbations (as long as they don't break the energy gap). This might be what 'topological' means.

Anyons in some topological orders carry exotic nonabelian statistics, that is, exchanging (braiding) anyons are nontrivial unitary operations rather than just phase factors as in the case of bosons or fermions. Mathematically, the excitations form an elegant structure called unitary modular tensor category.

On the other hand, anyons may undergo a process similar to Bose-Einstein condensation, called anyon condensation, to generate new topological orders.

Quantum Information

Another fascinating feature of topological orders is the presence of long-range entanglement. In some sense, normal phases including superfluids and Bose-Einstein condenses are classical because the quantum states are product states. In contrast, topological orders are not in product states due to long-range entanglement.

Therefore, we might say that topological orders are the first examples of truly quantum matter.

All matters are quantum, but some matters are more quantum than others. – Not George Orwell

On the other hand, our current understanding of quantum entanglement isn't quite satisfactory. We can determine whether a certain quantum state is entangled; we may even quantify entanglement by entanglement spectrum and entanglement entropy. But this description seems to be an oversimplification. It's like describing linear operators only by determinants, ignoring other delicate structures.

The structure of unitary modular tensor categories in topological orders implies a magnificent picture behind the scene. Quantum entanglement deserves a sophisticated mathematical structure, possibly geometric.

I believe further studies of topological orders would greatly deepen our understanding of quantum entanglement, which may even provide insights into the nature of spacetime.

Quantum Computation

Humankind has designed a whole range of different systems to extract the power of quantum parallelism, but quantum errors haunt these attempts.

The scheme of topological quantum computation is fundamentally different from the existing systems. Specifically, we take the Hilbert space of anyon fusions to be the logical space and take braidings to be quantum operations.

There are three main requirements for a scheme of quantum computation to be practical: ability to perform arbitrary quantum operations, robustness against errors, and scalability. The topological feature ensures robustness against errors; it is also shown that universal quantum computation can be effectively performed by braiding anyons (arxiv:quant-ph/0001108) Furthermore, the scheme is easily scalable.

Future Plans

I am deeply fascinated by topological orders and the possibility of topological quantum computation. At Fudan University, I joined Professor Yidun Wan’s group and conducted research under his supervision. I investigated the leakage problem in topological quantum computation and proposed a new design of qubits.

Currently, I’m trying to generalize the theory of anyon condensations to three-dimensional topological orders. It is proposed that Lagrangian algebras could describe anyon condensation in three-dimensional toric code (arxiv:2208.07865); I plan to first generalize the description to some simple models, for example $Z_n$ quantum doubles, then provide more general descriptions.

In the future, I would like to explore the following problems:

  • Classification of topological orders and theory of anyon condensation, especially in three dimensions
  • The interplay between topological orders and entanglement
  • Realization of topological quantum computation
